The Mechanics of Diamond Valuation: What Determines Value?
The value of a diamond is not arbitrary but is meticulously assessed based on a globally recognized set of criteria, primarily the "4 Cs," along with other critical factors. Understanding these elements is fundamental to appreciating the offer you receive when you seek "Cash for diamonds."

1. Carat Weight: This refers to the diamond's weight, not its size. One carat equals 200 milligrams. Larger diamonds are rarer and generally command higher per-carat prices, assuming other factors are equal.
2. Cut Quality: Often considered the most crucial of the 4 Cs, cut refers to how well a diamond's facets interact with light. An excellent cut maximizes brilliance (white light reflection), fire (dispersion of light into colors), and scintillation (sparkle). This is distinct from the diamond's shape (e.g., round, princess, emerald). Poor cut quality significantly diminishes a diamond's value.
3. Color Grade: Diamonds are graded on a color scale from D (colorless) to Z (light yellow or brown). Colorless diamonds are the rarest and most valuable. Subtle differences in color can have a substantial impact on price.
4. Clarity Grade: This measures the absence of inclusions (internal flaws) and blemishes (external imperfections). Graded from Flawless (FL) to Included (I), clarity significantly impacts a diamond's visual appeal and value. Most diamonds have some inclusions, and their size, number, position, and visibility determine the clarity grade.
5. Fluorescence: Some diamonds emit a visible glow when exposed to ultraviolet light. While often undetectable in normal lighting, strong fluorescence can sometimes give diamonds a milky or oily appearance, potentially reducing value.
6. Certification: Independent grading reports from reputable laboratories like GIA (Gemological Institute of America) or AGS (American Gem Society) provide an objective assessment of a diamond's 4 Cs and other characteristics. Certified diamonds often command higher confidence and value.
7. Market Demand and Trends: Like any commodity, diamond prices can fluctuate based on global supply, demand, and current fashion trends. Certain shapes or sizes may be more desirable at different times.
8. Overall Condition: Chips, abrasions, or previous repairs can affect a diamond's integrity and value. The condition of the setting (if applicable) and its material (e.g., platinum, 18k gold) also contribute to the overall item's worth.
