What Determines Your Diamond's Value?

The value of a diamond is complex and influenced by a multitude of interconnected factors. Understanding these elements is essential for any seller.

Carat Weight: This refers to the diamond's weight, not its size. One carat equals 200 milligrams. Generally, larger diamonds (higher carat weight) command higher prices per carat, assuming other factors are equal, due to their rarity.

Cut: This is arguably the most crucial of the 4Cs, referring to how well a diamond's facets interact with light. An excellent cut maximizes brilliance, fire, and scintillation. Poorly cut diamonds, even with high color and clarity, will appear dull. This is distinct from the diamond's shape (e.g., round, princess, oval).

Color: Diamonds are graded on a color scale from D (colorless) to Z (light yellow or brown). Colorless diamonds (D-F) are the most valuable due to their rarity and ability to transmit light without interference from yellow tints.

Clarity: This refers to the absence of inclusions (internal flaws) and blemishes (external flaws). The clarity scale ranges from Flawless (FL) to Included (I1, I2, I3). Fewer and less visible inclusions result in a higher clarity grade and greater value.

Fluorescence: Some diamonds emit a soft glow when exposed to ultraviolet light. While often imperceptible in normal lighting, strong fluorescence can sometimes give a diamond a milky or oily appearance, potentially impacting its value, especially for D-G color diamonds.

Certification: A diamond grading report from a reputable laboratory (such as GIA - Gemological Institute of America, AGS - American Gem Society, or IGI - International Gemological Institute) provides an objective assessment of a diamond's 4Cs and other characteristics. Certified diamonds generally command higher prices and instill greater confidence in buyers.

Setting Material & Design: If the diamond is set in jewelry, the value of the precious metal (e.g., 14K or 18K gold, platinum) and the craftsmanship of the setting contribute to the overall item's worth. Unique or antique settings can also add value beyond the intrinsic metal weight.

Market Demand & Rarity: Like any commodity, diamond prices are influenced by supply and demand. Certain sizes, shapes, or qualities may be more sought after at specific times, affecting their market value.

Overall Condition: The physical condition of the diamond and its setting is important. Chips, abrasions, or damage to the diamond itself can significantly reduce its value. Worn or damaged settings may also affect the overall offer.
